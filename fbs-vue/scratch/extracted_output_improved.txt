CHAPTER I INTRODUCTION - CONTEXT & PURPOSE
================================================================================
1.1 Project Context
The global tourism and hospitality industry is continuously evolving in response to technological advancements and increasing reliance on data-driven systems. In today’s digital environment, airline booking platforms, reservation systems, and customer management tools have become essential components of the sector. As a result, educational institutions must integrate innovative learning approaches, such as simulation-based platforms, to equip students with real-world competencies. Educational simulation systems provide an interactive, hands-on learning experience that allows students to practice decision-making, reservation processes, and customer interaction in a controlled and risk-free environment. This shift toward experiential and student-centered learning reflects modern trends in tourism education and prepares students to meet industry demands.[1]
Despite the growing importance of simulation technologies, there are still significant gaps in existing studies and implementations. Many available systems are either designed for commercial use or lack alignment with academic objectives. Existing research often focuses on general e-learning tools rather than specialized platforms tailored for tourism and hospitality training. Furthermore, limited attention has been given to the development of flight booking simulation systems that incorporate realistic scenarios, user interaction, and performance evaluation features specifically for students. This indicates a clear need for a more focused and education-oriented solution.[2]. 
In the current setting at Caraga State University Cabadbaran Campus (CSUCC), particularly within the College of Tourism and Hospitality Management (CTHM), teaching methods still rely heavily on theoretical instruction. While foundational knowledge is provided, students have limited exposure to actual airline booking procedures and real-world operational scenarios. The absence of an interactive simulation tool restricts their ability to apply learned concepts in practical situations. Consequently, students may struggle to understand complex booking systems, manage customer transactions, and develop the technical and interpersonal skills required in the tourism industry.[3]. 
To address these challenges, the proposed Flight Booking Simulation Platform offers an innovative and practical solution. The system is designed to replicate real-world airline booking environments, enabling students to perform tasks such as searching flights, managing reservations, and handling customer interactions. By providing a secure and controlled learning environment, the platform bridges the gap between theory and practice. It enhances students’ technical skills, improves their confidence, and prepares them for professional roles in the tourism and hospitality sector. Ultimately, this solution supports the university’s commitment to academic excellence and aligns with global trends in technology-driven education.


1.2 Purpose and Description:
The purpose of this capstone project is to create a Smart Flight Booking Simulation Platform that can be used as a training tool for CTHM students at CSUCC. The platform will replicate key components of real-world travel booking systems, including flight selection, student information input, seat reservation, payment simulation, booking confirmation and airline pricing
This simulation platform will not be connected to any live airline data or real-time booking systems, but will instead simulate the functionality and interface of true commercial flight booking platforms. The system is meant exclusively for educational purposes, with the goal of improving student capabilities in customer service, reservation handling, digital literacy, critical thinking and decision making skills in dynamic pricing scenarios.[4]


1.3 Objectives of the Study
The objective of this study is to create a flight booking simulation platform to help in the training and development of CTHM students at CSUCC.
Specifically, this study aims to:
Design a Flight Booking experience that simulates real-world reservation systems.
Develop modules that simulate flight selection, client data entry, seat mapping, and payment operations.
Create an educational learning tool with structured activities that students can complete to practice and apply booking skills.
Establish a Student Dashboard to track progress and access assigned activities.
Implement a Grading System to evaluate student performance based on the accuracy and completion of booking tasks.


1.4 Scope and Limitation of the Study 
Scope: 
This study focuses on developing a simulated airline booking and learning platform for CSUCC CTHM students. The system covers the end-to-end student journey through the following modules:
Booking Flow: Flight, seat, and add-on selection.
Data Handling: Personal and flight information input.
Transaction: Payment simulation and booking confirmation.
Check-in: Post-booking simulation.
LMS Integration: Educational tools for student learning and training.
Limitations:
 The platform is designed for educational simulation and will not be connected to live databases or airline systems.
Payment simulated real time and APIs use.
The study excludes integration with third-party applications or APIs used by commercial airlines.

4.3 The Developed System
This section presents the developed system for the Smart Flight Booking Simulation Platform, which is designed to provide students with a realistic and interactive learning environment that replicates actual airline reservation and departure control processes.
The process begins with user authentication, where students log into the system and are directed to the dashboard. From the dashboard, users can view and select available simulation activities. Upon selecting the flight booking simulation, the system provides instructions and requires an activity code to proceed, ensuring controlled access to the module.
The flight booking module allows students to simulate real-world airline reservation procedures. This includes searching for available flights, viewing search results, selecting a preferred flight, and entering passenger details. Users can also choose add-ons such as baggage options before proceeding to a booking review stage. A payment simulation is then conducted, after which the system confirms the booking and displays a success message.
In addition, the system incorporates a Check-in (Departure Control System) simulation module, which extends the learning experience beyond booking. In this module, users retrieve booking records through the flight manifest, verify passenger details, assign seats, manage baggage information, and generate a boarding pass. This feature enables students to understand post-booking operations commonly performed in online airline check-in.
Overall, the developed system successfully integrates both booking and check-in processes into a single platform. The system ensures a logical and step-by-step workflow, allowing students to gain hands-on experience in airline operations within a safe and controlled academic environment.

The study confirmed that CTHM students need hands-on training tools for airline booking systems, as traditional theoretical instruction limits real-world experience. The Smart Flight Booking Simulation Platform effectively addressed this gap by providing a realistic, interactive environment where students perform complete booking and check-in procedures.
The system achieved high ratings in functional suitability (4.80) and usability (4.53), demonstrating that it operates properly while remaining user-friendly. Students successfully applied theoretical knowledge to practical scenarios, mastering reservation handling and post-booking procedures.
Developed using Agile methodology, the system incorporated user feedback throughout the process, ensuring timely delivery of essential features. However, limitations remain: the platform supports only domestic flights without real-time data integration and lacks advanced features like rebooking, cancellations, and international travel options. These gaps affected the evaluation of security and maintainability.
Overall, the system successfully met its educational objectives, proving that simulation-based learning effectively enhances tourism  training at CTHM.
